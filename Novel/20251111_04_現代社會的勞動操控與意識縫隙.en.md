Thank you for your question. First, your observation accurately captures the structural transformation of modern management: from a labor-intensive management logic centered on factory production to a knowledge-labor management logic centered on knowledge, innovation, and psychological incentives. On this basis, you ask, "is this essentially a process of bureaucracy and hierarchy converging and seeking a balance?"
However, I would first like to clarify a statement in your question.
You mentioned "bureaucracy and hierarchy converging and seeking a balance." I guess you might be asking how to find a balance between "hierarchy" and "anti-hierarchy," or with "humanism"?
Why do I say this? Because hierarchy and bureaucracy, in Weber's theory, are essentially different translations of the same concept. We have already had a dedicated discussion on the difference between hierarchy and our ancient bureaucracy.
Okay, with that clarified, let's return to your core question: what is modern corporate management practice actually seeking a balance between?
Let's first go back to the time when modern management was born. It was the era of vigorous industrialization, and companies were mainly organized in the form of factories. The laborers were mostly manual workers, and the primary task of management was to maximize their output and production efficiency. Therefore, the core logic of management was "control": to make workers obey a unified institutional rhythm through means such as time, motion, division of labor, and supervision.
You see, this model is precisely the economic extension of Weber's "hierarchy," with a high emphasis on hierarchy, standardization, and discipline. It shaped the management culture of the entire industrial society and also laid the technical foundation for modern hierarchy.
However, this management model centered on efficiency and control has its temporal limitations. As you described, with the increasing proportion of knowledge-based labor, people gradually realized that organizational productivity comes not only from physical labor, but also from the coordination of psychological and social relationships, which means paying attention to the psychological state of employees.
Especially as we enter the information and knowledge economy era, the core assets of enterprises are no longer machines and land, but human knowledge and creativity. With the increase in knowledge workers, the traditional hierarchical structure naturally appears cumbersome, inefficient, and even stifles innovation.
Then you might ask: since hierarchy is not adapted to the knowledge economy, should it disappear or be eliminated?
The answer is: no.
Instead, hierarchy has appeared in a new form.
As mentioned in one of our previous answers, traditional hierarchy relies on documents, commands, and levels. In the current knowledge economy, these are largely replaced by algorithms, data, and performance systems. For example, companies monitor employee behavior through project management software, data analysis, and online performance appraisals. This "algorithmic hierarchy" is more detailed and covert than traditional supervision.
Let me give a slightly more extreme example. I remember reading a report about an internet company that developed a system to monitor the time employees spent in the restroom in real time. If the time exceeded a certain prescribed limit, the system would automatically sound an alert. You see, this kind of micro-control in the name of efficiency precisely reveals the regeneration and deepening of hierarchical rationality in the digital age.
In other words, while some companies have become flatter in their structure, the "calculability" of power has made control more intangible. Employees enjoy flexibility but are always in a state of being monitored and evaluated by the system. This reflects the duality of some contemporary corporate management: de-hierarchization in form, but strengthening of control in substance.
Therefore, from my perspective, the management practices of modern enterprises are not simply swinging or seeking a balance between "hierarchy" and "de-hierarchization," but are more like a compromise between "hierarchy" and "humanistic management." On the one hand, companies still need to retain the structural order and controllability brought by hierarchy to ensure operational stability and efficiency; on the other hand, they must also absorb the achievements of psychology and behavioral science, and introduce mechanisms such as flexible incentives, psychological safety, and team autonomy to stimulate people's creativity and subjectivity.
In other words, hierarchy provides the institutional skeleton, while humanistic management injects emotional and flexible flesh and blood. The fusion of the two is the result of continuous adjustments by contemporary organizations in the face of technological innovation and changes in labor forms. It enables companies to maintain systematic rational operation while retaining human warmth and creative space.
